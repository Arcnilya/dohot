\section{Discussion} \label{sec:disc}
In this section we compare our measurement results with that of previous
studies around anonymous \ac{dns} queries. We also compare the various schemes
in terms of threat model and deployability in addition to query latency.
Finally, the limitations of the study are listed, including the use of \texttt{NXDOMAIN}
lookups, caching effects, and Tor relay distribution in the world.

\subsection{Comparison with Related Work} \label{sec:compare}

Schmitt~\emph{et al.}~\cite{ODNS} compared traditional \ac{dns} resolution with
DNS resolution over the Tor network using the Alexa top 10,000 domains. Their
results showed a median resolution time of 31.31\,ms for traditional \ac{dns},
compared to 276.76\,ms over Tor. It is important to note that their evaluation
used a setup similar to \ac{dotor}, where DNS queries are resolved in plaintext
at the exit relay. In our measurements around \ac{dotor} with default torrc we
saw a median resolution time of 119\,ms, but managed to reduce it to 54\,ms by
tuning the circuits for latency.

Singanamalla~\emph{et al.}~\cite{singanamalla2020} evaluated the performance of
\ac{odoh} in a deployment setting, comparing it with several other protocols
including standard \ac{doh}, proxied \ac{doh}, various \ac{odoh}
configurations, DNSCrypt, and \ac{dohot}. They reported an average resolution
time of approximately 300\,ms for \ac{odoh}, while \ac{dohot} averaged
696.40\,ms. In contrast, our experiments showed a substantially lower average
for \ac{dohot} at 134.48\,ms. With a two-hop circuit using relays located in
the same country as the client, the average resolution time was further reduced
to 61.69\,ms.

Kurihara~\emph{et al.}~\cite{kurihara2023muodns} compared their
collusion-resistant approach to anonymous \ac{dns} queries: $\mu$ODNS, against
variations of \emph{Anonymous DNSCrypt}~\cite{adnscrypt}, as well as DNSCrypt
over Tor in terms of performance. For the Tor-based setting they had two
circuit configurations alongside a client in Japan (jp): (i) an entry in Japan
with middle and exit relays selected globally (i.e., \texttt{jp2any2any}), and
(ii) an entry in Japan with middle and exit relays located in East Asia and
East South Asia\footnote{limited to the following country-codes: cn, hk, jp,
kr, tw, kh, tp, id, my, ph, sg, th and vn}. The median query time of the first
setting was 1228.8\,ms and the second setting was 461.8\,ms. While we used
\ac{doh} over Tor instead of DNSCrypt, the results of our equivalent setting
were nowhere close to the high latency observed in their experiment (circuit
\texttt{xx2any2any} using torrc was 88\,ms, and carml+stem was 130\,ms). This
is likely due to the geographic distribution of Tor relays (further discussed
in Section~\ref{sec:limit}), where our experiments with a client in country x
greatly benefits compared to a client in Japan.

Xiao~\emph{et al.}~\cite{xiao2025} introduced a way to avoid proxies by having
the resolver resolve domains without knowing what they are using homomorphic
encryption. They highlighted different properties (on-path confidentiality,
client anonymity, and collusion resistance) for various approaches to resolving
\ac{dns} queries and compared them in performance. When comparing their
proposed solution (PDNS) against \ac{dohot}, we assume that they do not
configure any specific circuits (comparable to our \texttt{any2any2any}). They
reported a median query duration of around 450\,ms for \ac{dohot} with HTTPS
reuse while their own solution had a median of 208\,ms. Our results show a much
lower latency for the default configuration for \ac{dohot}, going as low as 
107\,ms (torrc \texttt{any2any2any}).

\subsection{Evaluation Dimensions of Anonymous DNS Schemes} \label{sec:eval}
We evaluate anonymous \ac{dns} schemes along three primary dimensions:
\emph{latency}, \emph{threat model}, and \emph{deployability}. The intent is to
highlight qualitative trade-offs rather than to claim absolute rankings, since
performance and privacy depend on the specific deployment parameters (e.g.,
vantage points, network conditions, and implementation choices). The choice of
scheme is dependent on the characteristics of the intended use case.

\subsubsection{Latency} Oblivious single-proxy schemes (\ac{odns} and
\ac{odoh}) generally achieve \emph{low} latency because they introduce only one
additional hop and modest cryptographic overhead. This comes at the cost of a
weaker threat model due to potential collusion between the proxy and the
resolver. Tor-based schemes incur \emph{higher} latency, mainly due to
multi-hop paths in the overlay network. Nevertheless, as shown in our
experiments, circuit selection and tuning can reduce the median latency by
roughly two thirds. Similarly to the Tor-based schemes, $\mu$ODNS use multiple
proxies to distribute trust during \ac{dns} resolution, so this also incurrs
additional latency. Most of the added delay for the \ac{pir}-based scheme comes
from the server‑side \ac{pir} computation, which performs homomorphic
evaluation over the resolver's encrypted cache. On cache misses, the client
performs iterative lookups directly to authoritative servers over \ac{doh},
which then forwards a cache update, optionally with a random delay to defeat
timing correlation.

\subsubsection{Threat Model} Tor-based schemes, $\mu$ODNS, and \ac{pir}
resolvers provide \emph{stronger} resistance to collusion compared to
single-proxy oblivious schemes. Tor distributes trust across independent
relays; $\mu$ODNS splits knowledge between resolvers; and \ac{pir} can offer
strong query confidentiality guarantees (information- theoretic or
computational, depending on the construction). In contrast, oblivious
single-proxy designs place more trust in a small number of entities, increasing
exposure under collusion assumptions. Stronger privacy, therefore, comes at
some latency penalty.

\subsubsection{Deployability} Tor-based solutions benefit from an
already-deployed overlay network and can tunnel \ac{dns} traffic (e.g.,
\ac{doh}/\ac{dot}) with minimal changes to the \ac{dns} ecosystem. Oblivious
single-proxy schemes and $\mu$ODNS typically require dedicated proxies or
modified resolvers to be widely useful, and \ac{pir}-based resolvers may
necessitate protocol changes and specialized server infrastructure. Regardless
of performance tradeoffs, practical deployment remains a bottleneck for many
proposed anonymity schemes in \ac{dns}. \ac{odoh} relies on the availability of
dedicated proxies and resolvers. In practice, deployment remains limited. 
%
The DNSCrypt project maintains curated, publicly accessible lists of \ac{odoh}
relays (proxies) and resolvers, which dnscrypt‑proxy uses to discover and
configure \ac{odoh} infrastructure~\cite{dnscrypt-git}. At the time of writing,
these lists contain only a single publicly listed \ac{odoh} proxy and seven
\ac{odoh}‑capable resolvers, indicating a very small and centralized deployment
footprint~\cite{dnscrypt-commit}. This limited ecosystem constrains both
scalability and trust distribution, as clients have little choice in proxy
selection and must place substantial trust in a small number of operators.
%
Lists of publicly available relays and resolvers for $\mu$ODNS are available;
however, the current deployment remains primarily
experimental~\cite{modns-git}. There are three relays and three resolvers, all
located in Tokyo. While this setup is sufficient to demonstrate feasibility and
performance characteristics, the lack of geographic and organizational
diversity limits the practicality of anonymity guarantees and underscores that
$\mu$ODNS, in its current form, is not yet deployed at a global scale. We did
not find any publicly available \ac{pir} resolvers online, and it seems the
work is framed as a proof‑of‑concept with a research prototype demo.

Table~\ref{tab:eval} shows an overview of the three dimensions for comparing
anonymous \ac{dns} schemes. The oblivious single-proxy schemes have low latency
but suffers from a relatively weak threat model and low deployability due to
specialized server infrastructure. The Tor-based schemes \emph{typically} have
high latency, although with a much stronger threat model as well as a readily
deployed infrastructure. The work in this study has been addressing the high
latency in Tor-based schemes since the threat model and deployability already
are relatively high. While 2-hop is weaker than the standard 3-hop circuits in
default Tor, it is still better than related schemes in anonymous \ac{dns}. The
$\mu$ODNS and \ac{pir}-based schemes have found a nice balance between latency
and threat model, but either require specialized infrastructure or changes to
\ac{dns} protocols and by extension the role of a recursive resolver. 

Regardless of performance trade-offs, practical deployment remains the
bottleneck for many anonymity schemes in \ac{dns}. The appropriate choice
depends on the intended use case and its constraints: latency budgets,
adversarial model, operational feasibility, and the availability of cooperating
infrastructure.


\subsection{Limitations} \label{sec:limit} 

Our experiments primarily measured \ac{dns} query latency using
\texttt{NXDOMAIN} lookups. This approach eliminates caching as a variable and
does not accurately reflect real-world usage, where cache hits are common. The
full resolution of an existing domain requires additional queries between the
resolver and name servers, increasing the round-trip time from client query to
resolver response. Depending on the measurement targets of the related works,
the \texttt{NXDOMAIN} queries could also explain some of our lower latency
results for comparison. Moreover, the improvement of routing and software stack
performance may have also contributed to the observed decrease in latency
compared to previous work in this area. Improvements to the performance of the
Tor network itself are particularly relevant~\cite{TorCongestion,
TorPerformance}.

The geographical location of the client is also tied to performance as
mentioned in Section~\ref{sec:compare}. Putting constraints on the location of
Tor relays may only be feasible in locations with enough availability. In
Figure~\ref{fig:map} we plot the geographical locations of available relays in
December 2025. The two most dense areas in the world are Europe and the U.S.,
this means that it might not be possible to select two relays within the same
country, optimizing for latency, outside these two areas. This is a limitation
of using Tor relays for this purpose, but the deployability of other collusion
resistant schemes are much worse, as shown in Table~\ref{tab:eval}.
