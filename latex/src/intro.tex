\section{Introduction} \label{sec:intro}

The \ac{dns} is a foundational Internet protocol for translating human-readable
domain names into resources, such as IP addresses~\cite{RFC1034,RFC1035}.
Whenever a user accesses a website or online service, a \ac{dns} query is
issued, and these queries have historically been sent in plaintext, exposing
users to surveillance, censorship, and profiling by network
adversaries~\cite{HoangNDKLMCGP21,KimZ15,PearceJLEFWP17}.

To address these concerns, privacy-enhancing protocols
\cite{RFC8484,RFC7858,RFC9250} have been standardized and deployed that encrypt
the communication between the client and the resolver, preventing on-path
network adversaries from inspecting or modifying the query contents. However,
these protocols do not anonymize the client: the resolver still sees both the IP
address of the client \emph{and} the contents of the query. This data is highly
sensitive as it can reveal interests, routines, and affiliations of individuals,
as well as operational details of organizations \cite{resolver-wiretap,RFC9076}.
Importantly, these risks affect not only end users, whose activities may be
exposed, but also resolver operators, who must safeguard their privileged
systems, and the data they process and collect. Even in jurisdictions with
strong privacy laws preventing the monetization of this type of data, there are
data breaches and leakage risks.

To break this direct link between client identity and \ac{dns} query content,
new protocols have been proposed. \emph{Oblivious} protocols introduce a
dedicated relay (or proxy) between the client and the resolver so that no
single entity can observe both ends of the query~\cite{RFC9230,ODNS}.
\emph{Tor-based} solutions use the Tor network~\cite{dingledine2004} to
separate the client identity from the query~\cite{DoHoT}. Another recent
proposal integrates \ac{pir} into \ac{dns} resolution by using homomorphic
encryption to process encrypted queries and return encrypted responses,
ensuring the resolver never learns the requested domain~\cite{xiao2025}.
Comparing these proposals solely in terms of query latency, which is a pain
point for both encrypted and anonymous \ac{dns} transport, places the Tor-based
solutions dead last~\cite{RFC9230,kurihara2023muodns,ODNS,xiao2025}.

In this paper, we compare different anonymous query schemes in terms of query
latency, threat model, and deployability concerns. We explore how Tor's
latency overhead can be reduced \emph{while at the same time} still provide
stronger privacy guarantees for \ac{dns} resolution compared to related work. 
The goal is \emph{not} to hide that the client is communicating with a \ac{dns}
resolver. The query content must be hidden from the relays while keeping the
client source IP hidden from the \ac{dns} resolver. That is why this study
investigates the viability of using two-hop Tor circuits and restricting relays
to geographic regions in close proximity to the client. Our main contributions
are as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Threat‑model grounding for anonymous \ac{dns}: We formalize the
        adversary (a resolver-side observer) and show that shortened two‑hop
        circuits (guard and exit) in Tor still prevent any single entity from
        linking client IP and query contents, providing strictly stronger
        protections than single‑proxy oblivious schemes under proxy-resolver
        collusion (Section~\ref{sec:threatmodel}).
    \item Measurement‑driven latency tuning of \acs{dohot}: Using programmable
        circuit construction (carml+Stem), we demonstrate that constraining two
        relays to a nearby region reduces median resolution time, from 107\,ms
        (p5--p95: 63--289\,ms) in the default configuration to 37\,ms (p5--p95:
        20--144\,ms) when using two‑hop circuits with co‑located relays, across
        1,000 queries per configuration (Section~\ref{sec:measurement}).
    \item Deployability and trade‑off analysis: We compare anonymous \ac{dns}
        schemes across latency, threat model, and deployability, positioning
        two‑hop localized \acs{dohot} as a practical middle ground: near
        \ac{odoh} latency while maintaining collusion resistance without new
        infrastructure (Section~\ref{sec:disc}).
\end{itemize}

Section~\ref{sec:background} presents background and related work. We define

measurements methodology, followed by measurement results in
Section~\ref{sec:measurement}. We discuss and ground our results in related work
