\section{Introduction} \label{sec:intro}

The \ac{dns} is a foundational Internet protocol for translating human-readable
domain names into resources, such as IP addresses~\cite{RFC1034,RFC1035}.
Whenever a user accesses a website or online service, a \ac{dns} query is
issued, and these queries have historically been sent in plaintext, exposing
users to surveillance, censorship, and profiling.

To address these concerns, privacy-enhancing protocols
\cite{RFC7858,RFC8484,RFC9250} have been introduced that encrypt the
communication between the client and the resolver, preventing on-path
adversaries from inspecting or modifying \ac{dns} traffic. However, they do not
anonymize the client: the resolver still sees both the IP address of the client
\emph{and} the contents of the query. In many parts of the world, resolvers
can, and often do, monetize or log this data without user consent. Even in
jurisdictions with strong privacy laws there are data breaches and leakage
risks.

To reduce this link between client identity and \ac{dns} queries, new protocols
have been proposed which introduce a cryptographic relay (or proxy) between the
client and the resolver so that no single entity can observe both ends of the
transaction~\cite{ODNS,RFC9230}. However, these systems depend on non-collusion
assumptions, involve dedicated infrastructure, and are therefore not yet widely
deployed.

An alternative that avoids introducing new infrastructure is to use the
existing Tor network~\cite{dingledine2004} to anonymize \ac{dns} queries. Tor
routes traffic through multiple volunteer-operated relays (typically three:
entry, middle, and exit), making it difficult for any observer to link a user
to their destination. Alec Muffett's proposal~\cite{DoHoT} exemplifies this
approach by tunneling encrypted \ac{dns} traffic through a Tor circuit. This
approach provides strong anonymity using an already operational global
anonymity network.

However, the latency introduced by Tor, due to three relays which in worst case
can be located on different continents, can make \ac{dns} resolution slow and
inconsistent. This study explores whether Tor's performance penalties can be
reduced without compromising privacy. Specifically, we investigate the
viability of using two-hop Tor circuits (entry and exit relays) for \ac{dns}
resolution, arguing that the third relay (the middle node) may not be essential
when using the threat model for anonymous \ac{dns} queries. Furthermore, we
examine whether circuit performance can be improved by restricting relays to
geographic regions in close proximation to the client.

We evaluate the latency reduction achieved by various methods and
configurations in Tor, highlighting their benefits and limitations in
comparison to other privacy-preserving \ac{dns} solutions. Our findings
demonstrate that, under realistic assumptions, a lightweight Tor-based approach
offers a compelling balance between privacy and performance, and it is ready to
use today with no additional infrastructure required.
