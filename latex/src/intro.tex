\section{Introduction} \label{sec:intro}

The \ac{dns} is a foundational Internet protocol for translating human-readable
domain names into resources, such as IP addresses~\cite{RFC1034,RFC1035}.
Whenever a user accesses a website or online service, a \ac{dns} query is
issued, and these queries have historically been sent in plaintext, exposing
users to surveillance, censorship, and profiling.

To address these concerns, privacy-enhancing protocols
\cite{RFC7858,RFC8484,RFC9250} have been standardized and deployed that encrypt
the communication between the client and the resolver, preventing on-path
adversaries from inspecting or modifying the query contents. However, these
protocols do not anonymize the client: the resolver still sees both the IP
address of the client \emph{and} the contents of the query. This data is highly
sensitive as it can reveal interests, routines, and affiliations of
individuals, as well as operational details of organizations
\cite{RFC9076,resolver-wiretap}. Importantly, these risks affect not only end
users, whose activities may be exposed, but also resolver operators, who must
safeguard the data they collect. Even in jurisdictions with strong privacy laws
preventing the monetization of this type of data, there are data breaches and
leakage risks.

To reduce this link between client identity and \ac{dns} queries, new protocols
have been proposed. The \emph{Oblivious} approaches introduce a cryptographic
relay (or proxy) between the client and the resolver so that no single entity
can observe both ends of the transaction~\cite{ODNS,RFC9230}. Taking this
concept to the extreme, \emph{Tor-based} approaches utilizes the popular
anonymity network~\cite{dingledine2004} to separate the client identity from
the query~\cite{DoHoT}. A more recent proposal involves integrating \ac{pir} to
\ac{dns} resolvers leveraging homomorphic encryption schemes~\cite{xiao2025}.

Comparing these approaches solely in terms of latency, which is a pain-point
for both encrypted and anonymous \ac{dns} transport, places the Tor-based
solutions dead last due to the multiple
relays~\cite{ODNS,RFC9230,xiao2025,kurihara2023muodns}. But the other
approaches are not without challenges, hindering deployment. Oblivious proxies
depend on non-collusion assumptions and involve setting up dedicated
infrastructure. \ac{pir} resolvers would circumvent the collusion problem but
require protocol changes. We argue that both threat model and deployability
should be added to performance when comparing these approaches. 

In this study we explore whether Tor's performance penalties can be reduced
without compromising privacy. Specifically, we investigate the viability of
using two-hop Tor circuits (entry and exit relays) for \ac{dns} resolution,
arguing that the middle relay may not be essential in terms of the threat model
for anonymous \ac{dns} queries. Additionally, we examine whether circuit
performance can be further improved by restricting relays to geographic regions
in close proximation to the client. Finally we compare various approaches to
anonymous \ac{dns} queries using three dimensions: latency, threat model, and
deployability. Our contributions are as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item we highlight that the reduced threat model, by modifying the
        circuits, still is stronger than the non-collusion assumptions by
        Oblivious approaches.
    \item we show that it is possible to cut the latency of Tor-based anonymous
        query approaches in half by using less relays in the circuits and
        placing those relays in relative close proximity to the client.
    \item we compare different anonymous query schemes in terms of performance,
        threat model, and deployability, emphasizing that more than performance
        should be taken into consideration when selecting a scheme for
        anonymizing \ac{dns} queries.
\end{enumerate}
