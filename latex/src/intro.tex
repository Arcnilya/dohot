\section{Introduction} \label{sec:intro}

The \ac{dns} is a foundational Internet protocol for translating human-readable
domain names into resources, such as IP addresses~\cite{RFC1034,RFC1035}.
Whenever a user accesses a website or online service, a \ac{dns} query is
issued, and these queries have historically been sent in plaintext, exposing
users to surveillance, censorship, and profiling by network
adversaries~\cite{HoangNDKLMCGP21,KimZ15,PearceJLEFWP17}.

To address these concerns, privacy-enhancing protocols
\cite{RFC8484,RFC7858,RFC9250} have been standardized and deployed that encrypt
the communication between the client and the resolver, preventing on-path
network adversaries from inspecting or modifying the query contents. However,
these protocols do not anonymize the client: the resolver still sees both the IP
address of the client \emph{and} the contents of the query. This data is highly
sensitive as it can reveal interests, routines, and affiliations of individuals,
as well as operational details of organizations \cite{resolver-wiretap,RFC9076}.
Importantly, these risks affect not only end users, whose activities may be
exposed, but also resolver operators, who must safeguard the data they collect.
Even in jurisdictions with strong privacy laws preventing the monetization of
this type of data, there are data breaches and leakage risks.

To break this link between client identity and \ac{dns} query content, new
protocols have been proposed. \emph{Oblivious} protocols introduce a dedicated
relay (or proxy) between the client and the resolver so that no single entity
can observe both ends of the query~\cite{RFC9230,ODNS}. \emph{Tor-based}
solutions use the Tor network~\cite{dingledine2004} to separate the client
identity from the query~\cite{DoHoT}. Another recent proposal involves
integrating \ac{pir} to \ac{dns} resolvers leveraging homomorphic encryption
schemes~\cite{xiao2025}. Comparing these proposals solely in terms of query
latency---which is a pain point for both encrypted and anonymous \ac{dns}
transport---places the Tor-based solutions dead
last~\cite{RFC9230,kurihara2023muodns,ODNS,xiao2025}.

In this paper, we compare different anonymous query schemes in terms of query
latency, threat model, and deployability concerns. We explore how Tor's latency
overhead can be reduced while providing ample privacy protections (compared to
related work) in the context of \ac{dns} resolution. We are not trying to hide
that the client is communicating with a \ac{dns} resolver. The query content
must be hidden from the relays while keeping the client source IP hidden from
the \ac{dns} resolver. That is why we investigate the viability of using
two-hop Tor circuits and restricting relays to geographic regions in close
proximity to the client. Our main contributions are as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item We show that it is possible to cut the average query latency of
        Tor-based solutions such as DoHoT in half (from 134$\pm$110ms to
        61$\pm$135ms) by using two-hop circuits and placing relays in relative
        close proximity to the client.
    \item An analysis that shows that despite weakening the protection from
        default Tor by changing circuit creation, Tor-based solutions provide
        stronger privacy-protections than oblivious protocols in addition to
        already being deployed.

\end{itemize}
