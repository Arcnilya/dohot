\section{Measurement Results} \label{sec:measurement} We performed our
measurements in four sets. The goal of the first set of measurements is to
compare the circuit configuration methods against each other. We want to
investigate performance differences between using \texttt{torrc} or
\texttt{carml+Stem} (Section~\ref{sec:measurement:p1}). The second set of
measurements explore the impact on latency when specifying the location of the
middle relay, something that only \texttt{carml+Stem} can do out of the two
methods (Section~\ref{sec:measurement:p2}). In the third set of measurements we
look at circuits without middle relays, also a capability we get from
\texttt{carml+Stem}
(Sections~\ref{sec:measurement:p3}--\ref{sec:measurement:p4}). Finally, we
compare the best performing configuration of the Tor-based approaches
(\ac{dohot} and \ac{dotor}) against the default \ac{dohot} setup and \ac{odoh}
with a proxy in Sweden, the same country as our client
(Section~\ref{sec:measurement:p5}).

\subsection{Query Time by Method (torrc vs. carml+Stem)}
\label{sec:measurement:p1}

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{img/p1.pdf}
    \caption{Comparing approaches for building circuits in Tor for \ac{dohot}
    in regards to query latency. On the left are the methods used for building
    circuits, and to the right are the circuits built. The circuit
    \texttt{any2any2any} means three unspecified relays, while
    \texttt{se2any2any} is a circuit with an entry relay in Sweden (se).}
    \label{fig:p1}
\end{figure}

We began by comparing the latency of circuits constructed using the standard
\texttt{torrc} configuration with those built using \texttt{carml+Stem}. As
shown in the box plot in Figure~\ref{fig:p1}, circuits created via
\texttt{torrc} outperformed those built with \texttt{carml+Stem}. This
performance gap is due to differences in relay selection. By default,
\texttt{torrc} selects relays based on weighted bandwidth, favoring faster, more
geographically optimal relays. In contrast, \texttt{carml+Stem} selects relays
uniformly at random within constraints such as location and relay type (e.g.,
exit or non-exit). Another takeaway from this measurement is that the latency is
reduced for both circuit-building methods when we place the entry and exit relays
closer to the client (i.e., \texttt{se2any2se}).

\subsection{Query Time by Circuit (Specific Middle)}
\label{sec:measurement:p2}

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{img/p2.pdf}
    \caption{Comparing \ac{dohot} circuits using carml+Stem to specify location
    of middle relay in regards to latency. The labels on the left are the
    circuits built with the country code for each relay. We include
    \texttt{se2any2se} from Figure~\ref{fig:p1} for comparison.}
    \label{fig:p2}
\end{figure}

One advantage of \texttt{carml+Stem} is controlling the middle relay placement.
By specifying the middle relay's location, we can reduce the geographical
spread of circuits, and thus reduce the latency of the queries.
Figure~\ref{fig:p2} shows a notable reduction in latency when the middle relay,
in addition to the guard and exit relays, is constrained to be in the same
country as the client (see se2any2se versus se2se2se). As expected, reducing
the geographical distance between relays also improves performance. The main
takeaway here is that by placing all three relays in close proximity to the
client we can reduce the query latency from a median of 200ms (see
\texttt{carml+Stem}, \texttt{any2any2any} in Figure~\ref{fig:p1}) to 60ms
(\texttt{se2se2se} in Figure~\ref{fig:p2}) with much less spread.

\subsection{Query Time by Circuit (Without Middle)}
\label{sec:measurement:p3}

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{img/p3.pdf}
    \caption{Comparing \ac{dohot} circuits without middle relays using
    carml+Stem in regards to latency.}
    \label{fig:p3}
\end{figure}

We used \texttt{carml+Stem} to construct two-hop circuits, consisting
only of an entry and an exit relay, a configuration not supported by
\texttt{torrc}. As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:p3}, this further reduces
latency. The best performance was observed when both relays were in close
proximity to the client with a median query time of 40ms. 

\subsection{Query Time by Circuit (DoTor carml+Stem)}
\label{sec:measurement:p4}

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{img/p4.pdf}
    \caption{Comparing \ac{dotor} circuit configurations using carml+Stem,
    showing that the query time is reduced by shortening the circuit and
    selecting relays in close proximation to the client.}
    \label{fig:p4}
\end{figure}

We also ran measurements with tuned circuits for \ac{dotor}. Due to the two
main factors affecting the latency of \ac{dotor} being the latency of the
circuit itself as well as the latency of the \ac{dns} query by the exit relay
it stands to reason that a shorter circuit results in lower latency. A circuit
with all nodes in the same country as the client should lower latency and
removing the middle relay further so. This expected behaviour is consistent
with the results shown in Figure~\ref{fig:p4}.

\subsection{Query Time by Approach}
\label{sec:measurement:p5}

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{img/p5.pdf}
    \caption{Comparing the default torrc configuration (any2any2any) of DoHoT and DoTor with
    DoHoT (se2se), DoTor (se2se), as well as ODoH with a proxy in Sweden.}
    \label{fig:p5}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:p5} presents a side-by-side comparison between the default
configuration of \ac{dohot} (using torrc) introduced by Alex
Muffet~\cite{DoHoT}, tuned circuits for \ac{dohot} and \ac{dotor}, as well as
\ac{odoh} with a Swedish proxy. Even though the improved query latency of
\ac{dohot} and \ac{dotor} is worse than \ac{odoh}, we managed to cut the
latency in half compared with the default Tor configuration. At the same time,
we maintain a stronger threat model than single collusion-prone oblivious
proxies.
