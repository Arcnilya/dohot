\section{Threat Model} \label{sec:threatmodel}

The threat model for anonymous \ac{dns} queries considers a passive adversary
with access to query information at the target recursive resolver. The
objective is to anonymize the client's identity (IP address) so that it cannot
be linked to the issued query.

Oblivious approaches such as \ac{odoh} rely on a single-proxy model whose
security hinges entirely on a \emph{non-collusion assumption}: the proxy that
sees the client's IP must not cooperate with the resolver that sees the
plaintext query. If these entities collude, the client’s privacy is immediately
compromised. This assumption is hard to verify or enforce in practice,
especially if both parties fall under the same operational or legal domain.

In contrast, the Tor network provides a stronger and more flexible threat
model. Tor achieves anonymity by routing traffic through multiple independent,
volunteer-operated relays and by frequently rotating circuits. Privacy is
preserved as long as not \emph{all} relays on a circuit collude. The default
use-case for Tor does \emph{not} protect against a global adversary, who would
find out both the source and the destination.

For \ac{doh} queries over Tor, the entry relay can observe the client's IP and
encrypted traffic but not the \ac{dns} query itself. The middle relay only sees
encrypted traffic without access to either the client IP, resolver IP, or the
query. The exit relay only sees encrypted traffic and the target \ac{doh}
resolver. The \ac{doh} resolver sees the decrypted \ac{dns} queries and the
exit's IP, but never the client's IP. Even if the Tor circuit is shortened
(e.g., by removing the middle relay), the design still ensures that no single
entity can observe both the client's identity and the content of their queries
simultaneously. Even if all relays are colluding in this setting, they still do
not have access to the query since it is encrypted when sent to the \ac{doh}
resolver. Thus, Tor's threat model remains stronger than that of single
oblivious proxies, which fail completely under collusion between the two
involved parties.
